On the storage team, we used the following questions to guide our development: <br> <br> - what form of heat storage (chemical, sensible, latent) is the most efficient for this project? <br> - what heat storage material should be used, considering safety, heat capacity, heat of fusion, corrisivity, and affordability? <br> - how can we control the level of heat being applied to the cooking surface? <br> Storage team's pin up #1 can be found here (references linked within Google Slide): https://docs.google.com/presentation/d/1L8QO2hFe0YEIImoED_7eao3nOXle57VCv-ufpZuIcac/edit?usp=sharing <br> <br> After seeing the other groups' pin ups and discussing how all of our work connects, we concluded that our goal is to provide 400F-450F to the cooking surfacce, with a 100F minimum. We also considered making a partitioned stovetop where each section has a different temperature. This would circumvent the problem of long wait-times when a dish has multiple stages of cooking with different temperatures. <br> <br> We decided that latent heat storage is the most effective method; sensible heat storage would have a varying operating temperature, which can be less predictable and cause waste, and chemical heat storage's high maximum temperature makes execution harder and potentially more dangerous. One large contender for storage material is nitrate eutectic, which is likely available in the MIT MakerSpace and is already commonly used for thermal storage. <br> <br> Latent heat storage means investing thermal energy into the latent heat of a phase change material (PCM). Within a certain range of stored heat, the PCM will not increase in temperature, but will instead store this energy towards changing phases (in our case, melting a solid into a liquid). As the liquid cools down and solidifies again, it releases energy.
